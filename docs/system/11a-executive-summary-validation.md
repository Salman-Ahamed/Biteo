# Executive Decision Summary: FastBite Pilot Recommendation

**Assessment Date:** May 18, 2026  
**Role:** Corporate Strategy Analyst  
**Overall Recommendation:** Conditional GO for a tightly scoped Phase 0 pilot

---

## Executive Judgment

This summary should support a leadership decision on whether FastBite has enough market support to justify a pilot. The answer is yes, but only in a narrower sense than the previous draft suggested.

The strongest externally supported conclusions are that UK market concentration increased after DoorDash's acquisition of Deliveroo, incumbent platforms are actively investing in merchant tooling, and merchant software pricing is competitive enough that FastBite will need to prove workflow value rather than rely on generic dashboard functionality. The weaker parts of the prior summary were the precise numbers, aggressive timelines, and binary conclusions that were not supported by the longer evidence base.

The right board-level framing is therefore:

- there is enough evidence to justify a learning-focused pilot
- there is not enough evidence to justify aggressive scaling assumptions
- the pilot should be judged on evidence generation, not on feature delivery alone

---

## What Leadership Can Rely On

### 1. Market concentration increased materially

DoorDash announced an agreement to acquire Deliveroo in May 2025 at about USD3.9 billion / GBP2.9 billion, and later completed the acquisition in October 2025. That materially changes the competitive backdrop and supports the conclusion that FastBite faces larger, better-capitalized incumbents than many earlier assumptions implied.

### 2. Incumbents are investing in merchant-side tooling

DoorDash's May 2026 merchant product launch included AI-assisted onboarding, merchant content tools, website generation, and direct-ordering support. This supports the view that merchant operations are now a live product battleground.

### 3. Operational automation remains a priority for major platforms

Uber Eats and Starship announced a UK robot-delivery pilot in November 2025, starting in Leeds. That does not prove broad automation economics, but it does support the conclusion that incumbents continue to invest in operational control and experimentation.

### 4. Merchant software pricing pressure is real

Square's UK pricing shows free entry points and paid plans from GBP29+ per month. This is important because it means FastBite must justify any premium price with measurable operating value.

### 5. The operational-reliability wedge remains the best strategic angle

The longer report supports a merchant-first thesis focused on order orchestration and peak-hour workflow improvement more strongly than a thesis centered on commission relief or consumer acquisition.

---

## What Should Be Softened, Removed, Or Left Open

### Too strong

1. "Merchant pain is REAL" overstates the current evidence. Merchant pain is directionally supported, but this review still lacks quantified UK-specific evidence on tablet overload, missed orders, or peak-time workflow failure.
2. "Creates UK duopoly" is too strong without current market-share evidence. The market is more concentrated, but the two-player framing remains an interpretation rather than a verified fact.
3. "Your 12-18 month head start window is now 6-9 months" is not evidenced and should be removed.
4. "GO" as a blanket recommendation is too strong. The defensible recommendation is a conditional pilot, not full confidence in the broader plan.

### Too vague

1. "B2B market exists" is directionally true, but the startup funding examples used previously were not sufficiently verified for executive use.
2. "Operational focus wins" is strategically plausible, but it should be tied to a clearer implication: FastBite should position around workflow reliability and merchant usability.
3. "Fragmented operations layer = real wedge for growth" needs to be reframed as a hypothesis worth testing rather than a proven growth thesis.

### Too compressed

1. Direct-order adoption assumptions were compressed into precise CAC, repeat-visit, and direct-mix claims that were not externally verified.
2. Delivery economics were compressed into specific margin and utilization figures that the longer report itself treated as uncertain.
3. The scorecard used numeric confidence values that implied more precision than the evidence supports.
4. The competitive urgency section converted a real risk into a countdown model that was not evidence-based.

---

## Planning Assumptions That Still Need Proof

The following recommendations are directionally reasonable but should be treated as planning assumptions, not externally supported requirements:

1. A 12-week pilot may be sensible, but this review did not verify that 12 weeks is the only defensible duration.
2. A larger merchant pilot cohort may improve pattern recognition, but the claim that 8-10 merchants is a defensible minimum is too strong.
3. Transport hubs and hospital clusters appear more defensible than office-only zones, but the zone ranking is still a hypothesis until local demand data is collected.
4. The proposed pricing stack of platform fee, per-order orchestration fee, and analytics add-on is a plausible model, but the exact price points are not externally supported.
5. Twelve-month merchant contracts may help retention, but the summary should not present them as a proven moat.
6. The recommendation to accelerate direct marketplace connectors is strategically reasonable, but it should follow proof that merchants value the workflow enough to justify the integration cost.

---

## How Leadership Should Frame The Decision

The executive narrative should read less like a pitch and more like a gated investment memo.

It should:

1. Start with the decision: approve a narrow pilot or do not.
2. Separate what is externally supported from what remains unproven.
3. Emphasize that the pilot's purpose is evidence generation across the biggest unknowns: merchant workflow pain, direct-order conversion, zone demand stability, and delivery economics.
4. Replace hard numeric claims with explicit learning questions unless there is named evidence behind them.
5. Remove sensational framing such as countdowns to feature parity, unsupported budget certainty, or highly specific failure scenarios that imply false precision.

## Recommended Executive Framing

### Executive View

FastBite remains worth testing, but the evidence supports a narrower conclusion than the previous draft implied. The market does not appear wide open. It appears concentrated, competitive, and increasingly focused on merchant tooling.

The strongest case for FastBite is not that it will outcompete large marketplaces on consumer reach or price. The strongest case is that some merchants may still value a product that reduces operational friction across ordering channels, improves peak-hour execution, and creates a path toward merchant-owned repeat demand.

That is enough to justify a Phase 0 pilot. It is not enough to justify aggressive scaling assumptions before pilot evidence exists.

### What Leadership Can Treat as Decision-Relevant Today

1. Competitive concentration increased after DoorDash's acquisition of Deliveroo.
2. Merchant tooling is an active competitive frontier, not a neglected niche.
3. Merchant software pricing remains competitive, so FastBite must prove clear operating value.
4. The operational-reliability wedge is more defensible than a commission-savings story.

### What Leadership Should Treat as Unproven

1. How severe merchant workflow pain is in the specific UK zones FastBite wants to enter.
2. How much direct-order migration is realistically achievable in an early pilot.
3. Whether one zone type is clearly superior without local demand evidence.
4. Whether delivery-service economics can support reliable operations without eroding margins.
5. Whether merchants will pay the proposed price structure after initial setup and support effort are included.

### Implications for Phase 0

The pilot should be approved only as an evidence-generation exercise with tightly defined questions.

**Phase 0 should test:**

1. Whether merchants use the workflow consistently during peak periods.
2. Whether order handling becomes measurably more reliable.
3. Whether repeat customers can be shifted toward merchant-owned channels at any meaningful rate.
4. Whether delivery supply can be managed without unacceptable cost volatility.
5. Whether merchants show willingness to continue after setup support falls away.

**Phase 0 should not assume in advance:**

1. A specific direct-order mix target.
2. A specific merchant price band.
3. A specific delivery contribution margin.
4. A precise competitive response timeline.
5. A fixed expansion path beyond the pilot.

### Revised Decision Scorecard

| Topic                                         | Evidence strength | Decision implication                      | Recommended stance            |
| --------------------------------------------- | ----------------- | ----------------------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Market concentration after DoorDash-Deliveroo | High              | Raises incumbent-response risk            | Incorporate into strategy now |
| Merchant operations as a viable wedge         | Medium            | Worth piloting                            | Test directly in Phase 0      |
| Direct-order conversion economics             | Low               | Do not build plan around assumed adoption | Treat as pilot learning goal  |
| Zone and daypart demand stability             | Low               | Avoid broad geographic assumptions        | Validate with local data      |
| Delivery supply economics                     | Low               | Avoid broad service promises              | Use constrained pilot design  |
| Merchant willingness to pay FastBite pricing  | Low to medium     | No pricing commitment yet                 | Validate after workflow proof |

### Recommendation

**Recommendation:** Conditional GO for a tightly scoped Phase 0 pilot.

**Conditions for approval:**

1. Approve the pilot as a test of the business thesis, not as confirmation that the business model is proven.
2. Use stage gates tied to merchant usage, workflow improvement, and post-pilot willingness to pay.
3. Keep zone choice evidence-led, with preference for dense areas that are less dependent on office-only demand if such zones are available.
4. Keep pricing, delivery model, and integration roadmap flexible until early evidence exists.
5. Require a formal review at the end of Phase 0 before approving expansion.

### Final Assessment

FastBite still looks like a legitimate pilot candidate, but not a high-certainty growth plan. The market signal is strong enough to justify a disciplined test. The evidence is not strong enough to justify aggressive time-to-scale, hard commercial targets, or highly specific operating assumptions.

Leadership should therefore fund a narrow pilot if the objective is to answer the core unknowns quickly and honestly. Leadership should not treat the current evidence base as sufficient proof that the post-pilot model, pricing, or expansion plan is already proven.
