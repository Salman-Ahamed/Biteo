# 01. Executive Summary

## Purpose

Use this file to answer one question quickly: is FastBite realistic, and in what form?

## One-Page Summary

- FastBite is plausible in the UK if it starts as a merchant operating platform for independent and small-chain quick-service restaurants, not as a broad consumer marketplace.
- The strongest near-term entry point is a system that unifies marketplace orders, first-party reordering, kitchen operations, and short-radius dispatch control.
- The most practical launch conditions are dense urban micro-zones with short delivery radii, concentrated merchant supply, and repeat demand; breakfast and lunch should be treated as a testable hypothesis, not a starting fact.
- The weakest strategy is trying to outspend Uber Eats, Deliveroo, and Just Eat on consumer acquisition from day one in a market where incumbents already have scale, logistics density, and merchant relationships.
- The commercial wedge should be operational clarity first, measurable margin improvement second, and direct-customer retention third.
- The trust claim only works if outages, provider failures, and sync problems degrade visibly and recover safely rather than producing false confirmations.

## Core Decision

FastBite should be positioned as:

**The merchant operating and reordering layer for UK quick-service restaurants in dense local zones.**

This is stronger than positioning it as a cheaper food-delivery app, but it only works if FastBite is materially easier to use, more operationally useful, or better aligned to merchant economics than existing order-management and white-label alternatives.

## Critical Assumptions

- merchants will pay for workflow software that reduces missed orders, improves peak-time control, and gives clearer channel economics
- target merchants are not already well served by incumbent order-management, aggregation, or white-label tools
- the first zone can support short delivery radii and viable contribution margins without heavy consumer subsidy
- direct reordering can be grown compliantly and economically from customers first discovered through marketplaces

## What FastBite Must Solve

1. Replace multiple tablets and fragmented tools with one operating surface.
2. Improve merchant margin by creating a practical path from marketplace discovery to first-party repeat ordering.
3. Improve peak-time execution through better kitchen, prep-time, and dispatch coordination.
4. Preserve enough merchant control over repeat demand, customer data, and channel mix to justify adoption.
5. Show merchants where margin is being won or lost across direct, marketplace, and delivery channels.
6. Protect trust during incidents by keeping orders durable, exposing degraded state, and avoiding false success claims.

## What FastBite Should Not Be At Launch

- another citywide marketplace
- an undifferentiated copy of existing aggregation or white-label software
- a WhatsApp-only commerce flow
- a blanket 30-minute promise across all zones and times
- an AI-heavy product before the core workflow is reliable

## Best Initial Form

- merchant-first operating system for independent and small-chain QSRs
- first-party web ordering and reordering, with messaging used for service and retention rather than as the sole order surface
- marketplace aggregation into one queue
- KDS, prep-time control, and store availability management
- short-radius dispatch orchestration, likely with partner fleet support at first
- focused pilot in one dense micro-zone with a clearly defined merchant cohort

## Win Conditions

- merchants prefer the FastBite dashboard over their current multi-tablet setup and use it daily
- prep-time accuracy, order acceptance, and on-time performance improve during the target peak windows
- direct reorder share grows over time without heavy paid acquisition
- the first micro-zone shows acceptable contribution margin after delivery, support, and merchant success costs
- merchant retention proves the product is operationally useful, not just interesting
- merchants see enough value versus incumbent aggregation and white-label options to keep paying

## Immediate Decisions To Lock

1. first launch zone
2. first merchant profile
3. first marketplace ingestion path
4. first courier supply model
5. first clear point of differentiation versus Deliverect, Flipdish, and incumbent platform tools

## Open Questions

- Which micro-zone has the best mix of density, merchant concentration, and repeat demand?
- Are breakfast and lunch genuinely the best initial demand windows, or will evening and end-of-week demand produce better density and unit economics?
- Should the MVP use a third-party aggregator, direct integrations, or both?
- Should delivery supply start with partner fleets, scheduled riders, or a mixed model?
- Which merchant segment is least well served by current aggregation and first-party ordering tools?
