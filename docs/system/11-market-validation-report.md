# 11. Market Assessment and Pilot Implications

**Date:** May 2026  
**Prepared for:** FastBite Executive Team  
**Status:** Decision support memo

---

## Executive Summary

FastBite merits a narrowly scoped pilot, but the market case is tighter than earlier drafts implied. The merchant-first operational wedge remains more defensible than a pure consumer-acquisition or commission-savings play, but several earlier claims should remain assumptions until local evidence exists.

### What leadership can rely on now

- DoorDash announced an agreement to acquire Deliveroo in May 2025 at roughly $3.9 billion / GBP2.9 billion and completed the acquisition in October 2025.
- Large platforms are investing in merchant tooling, not just consumer growth. DoorDash launched AI-powered merchant onboarding and direct-ordering tools in May 2026.
- Uber Eats and Starship announced a UK robot-delivery launch in November 2025, beginning in Leeds. This is evidence of continued operational experimentation by incumbents, not proof of broad market rollout.
- Official UK pricing from Square shows low-friction merchant software entry points, including free tiers and paid plans from GBP29+/month. That means FastBite must prove value above baseline software expectations.

### What still needs local proof

- Merchant pain from fragmented order channels is directionally supported by the growth of integration and merchant-ops tooling, but this report does not yet cite strong UK-specific quantified evidence.
- Direct ordering remains strategically attractive for merchants, but claims about required direct-order mix, CAC, and conversion timelines were not confirmed with named UK evidence.
- Delivery orchestration economics are likely thin, but the original report's precise per-order margin statements were not supported by named evidence.

### What should stay as assumptions or be removed

- The Grubhub fee-waiver example is US-specific and should not be used as proof of UK merchant economics.
- The report's specific startup funding examples were not all verifiable from clearly named sources during this review and should not carry strategic weight until sourced.
- The claim that the UK is now effectively a DoorDash-Deliveroo plus Uber Eats duopoly is an interpretation, not a settled fact; Just Eat should not be written off without fresh data.
- Specific claims about onboarding cost, monthly support cost, direct-order mix thresholds, order-volume targets, and merchant-density thresholds were not externally verified.

## 2. Decision Question

This document is intended to answer a practical question for leadership: whether FastBite has enough real market support to justify a tightly scoped pilot in the UK food-delivery and restaurant-operations market.

That purpose is appropriate. The main quality issue in the original version was not the overall direction of travel; it was that the document often converted reasonable hypotheses into factual-seeming statements without maintaining a clear boundary between:

- externally verified evidence
- interpretation of that evidence
- recommendations for FastBite's plan

---

## 3. Market Findings

### 3.1 Findings leadership can use now

#### A. Consolidation is real and recent

DoorDash's agreement to acquire Deliveroo was announced on 6 May 2025 at about $3.9 billion / GBP2.9 billion, and the acquisition was later completed in October 2025. That is a real shift in platform structure and supports the report's broader conclusion that independent entrants face larger, better-capitalized incumbents.

**Interpretation:** Market concentration increased, and FastBite should assume that at least one major incumbent has more scale, more merchant reach, and more capital than when many legacy assumptions about UK food-delivery competition were formed.

**Recommendation:** Keep the strategic implication, but avoid overstating the exact post-deal competitive structure unless current market-share evidence is added.

#### B. Incumbents are investing in merchant tooling

DoorDash's May 2026 merchant-product launch included AI-assisted onboarding from merchant websites, AI image tools, marketing automation, and website generation for merchants' direct-ordering channels.

**Interpretation:** FastBite should assume that merchant experience, onboarding, and direct-order enablement are active battlegrounds. The market is not standing still.

**Recommendation:** Compete on workflow reliability, execution speed, and merchant service quality rather than assuming incumbents will ignore merchant tooling.

#### C. UK robot-delivery pilots continue, but pilots are not proof of defensible economics

Uber Eats and Starship announced a UK robot-delivery launch in November 2025, starting in Leeds.

**Interpretation:** Incumbents continue to test automation and operational-control levers.

**Recommendation:** Treat this as evidence of incumbent experimentation, not as evidence that autonomous delivery materially changes FastBite's near-term position.

#### D. Merchant software pricing pressure is real

Square's UK pricing shows free entry tiers and paid plans from GBP29+/month, including restaurant-specific packages.

**Interpretation:** Merchants are accustomed to software products with low entry prices and modular upsell structures. That does not invalidate FastBite pricing, but it means pricing power must be earned through measurable operational outcomes rather than generic dashboard functionality.

**Recommendation:** Position any premium price around quantifiable operational value such as fewer missed orders, faster order handling, or stronger reorder retention.

### 3.2 Areas that still need pilot proof

#### A. Merchant operational pain from fragmented channels

This is likely true and commercially important. The problem is supported indirectly by the existence of multi-channel order-management vendors and by incumbent investments in merchant ops tools. What is missing is a current UK source that quantifies how common this pain is among target merchants or how severe the failure mode becomes during peak periods.

**Interpretation:** The claim is viable as a working thesis, but it should be treated as a pilot hypothesis to test with interviews and operating data.

#### B. Direct ordering is harder than assumed

This claim is directionally convincing, but the original report became too precise in places. The review did not confirm named evidence for the proposed direct-order mix thresholds, conversion timelines, or CAC assumptions.

**Interpretation:** It is safer to say that direct-order economics usually require repeat traffic, merchant activation, and smooth checkout, and that the economics should be tested in pilot rather than assumed.

#### C. Dense micro-zones are more workable than wide geographies

This is operationally plausible and consistent with last-mile economics, but the report's specific thresholds such as restaurant density, radius, and daily order volume need evidence or should be relabeled as planning assumptions.

### 3.3 Points to remove, replace, or narrow

#### A. Grubhub fee-waiver evidence

This is not a good proof point for UK market dynamics. It is a US example and does not validate current UK merchant economics.

#### B. Toast as a UK pricing comparator

Toast is a meaningful restaurant-tech company, but the pricing page reviewed is US-oriented. It should not be used as a direct UK merchant pricing signal without a UK-specific source.

#### C. "Regulatory stability" in rider classification

That wording is too confident. The right framing is that UK worker-classification risk remains live and should be scenario-modeled rather than dismissed.

## 5. Statements To Soften Or Remove

The following statements should be corrected, removed, or softened:

1. Replace claims of a fully formed UK duopoly with wording such as: "DoorDash's acquisition of Deliveroo materially increases concentration, but current market-share evidence is still needed before describing the UK as a two-player market."
2. Replace hard claims about commission dynamics with wording such as: "Third-party commissions remain a merchant concern, but this review did not verify current UK-wide pricing pressure or widespread fee waivers from named UK sources."
3. Replace claims about direct-order thresholds with wording such as: "Direct-order economics should be treated as a pilot metric rather than assumed in advance."
4. Remove unsupported cost figures for onboarding, support, and delivery contribution margin unless they are tied to named internal or external evidence.
5. Reframe pilot structure as a recommendation rather than as a fixed market conclusion.
6. Reframe employment and rider-regulation commentary as uncertainty, not stability.
7. Remove or source the funding examples before using them as proof of market attractiveness.

## 6. Missing Evidence To Add Later

The report would become materially stronger with the following external evidence:

1. Current UK market-share or order-share data covering Deliveroo, Uber Eats, and Just Eat after the Deliveroo transaction.
2. UK merchant interviews or survey data quantifying pain from multi-channel tablet overload, manual re-entry, missed orders, or delayed kitchen flow.
3. UK-specific evidence on direct-order adoption rates, reorder behavior, or loyalty conversion among independents and small chains.
4. Current courier-cost benchmarks for UK dense urban zones, including partner-fleet versus gig-supply tradeoffs.
5. Zone-level evidence on breakfast and lunch demand patterns using office-footfall, hospital-shift, or campus-traffic data.
6. UK examples of merchants paying for operations software specifically because it reduces order-handling complexity, not just because it replaces a POS.

## 7. Planning Adjustments

1. Keep the merchant-ops thesis, but downgrade unsupported numeric precision.
2. Separate every major section into evidence, interpretation, and recommendation.
3. Use UK-specific evidence where geography matters, especially for pricing, regulation, demand shape, and delivery economics.
4. Treat launch-zone selection, pilot size, and revenue design as assumptions to test, not fixed conclusions.
5. Shift the core value proposition from "cheaper delivery" to "operational control and merchant workflow reliability" because that angle is better supported by current competitive evidence.

## 8. Recommended Framing

### Revised Executive View

FastBite remains potentially viable as a merchant-first restaurant operations product, but the strongest externally supported case is narrower than the original draft implied. The most defensible reading of the evidence is not that marketplaces are failing merchants across every dimension, but that large platforms are consolidating while simultaneously improving merchant tooling. That raises the bar for a new entrant.

The strongest available wedge is operational reliability for busy restaurants: unify order intake, reduce channel friction, improve peak-hour execution, and create a path to direct ordering where repeat demand already exists. The weakest wedge is pure commission relief. That message should be explicit.

### Revised Market Position

FastBite should be framed as an operational command layer for merchants rather than a generic challenger to major marketplaces. The market evidence supports three practical conclusions:

1. Merchant workflow pain is important enough to test, even if the current report lacks quantified UK proof.
2. Incumbents are already moving into merchant automation, so FastBite cannot rely on feature novelty.
3. FastBite's pilot should prove a small number of measurable merchant outcomes rather than try to validate a broad strategy all at once.

### Revised Implications for the Plan

#### What FastBite can reasonably claim today

- The UK market became more concentrated after DoorDash's Deliveroo acquisition.
- Merchant tooling is a live competitive frontier.
- Merchants already buy low-friction operational software, but price sensitivity remains real.
- A focused pilot around order orchestration and merchant workflow improvement is strategically coherent.

#### What FastBite should still treat as assumptions

- That direct ordering will quickly reach a specific order mix
- That merchants will readily pay a specific monthly fee band
- That one launch-zone archetype is clearly superior without local demand evidence
- That rider economics or regulation are stable enough to rely on broad delivery guarantees

### Revised Pilot Recommendation

Proceed only with a narrowly framed pilot whose purpose is evidence generation, not scale signaling.

**Pilot objective:** determine whether FastBite can improve merchant operations enough for merchants to use the product consistently during peak periods and continue paying after initial setup.

**Suggested pilot scope:** one dense zone, a small cluster of merchants, one or two integration paths, and a short list of measurable merchant outcomes.

**Suggested pilot questions:**

1. Does daily active merchant use hold during peak service periods?
2. Does the workflow reduce missed orders, re-entry, or order-handling confusion?
3. Do merchants see enough operational benefit to tolerate setup effort and ongoing fees?
4. Do any repeat customers migrate to merchant-owned ordering without excessive merchant prompting?
5. Are delivery-service economics manageable when the zone, weather, and daypart are constrained?

### Revised Strategic Assessment

FastBite appears viable only if it proves a merchant-operations advantage that is both measurable and hard to abandon. The evidence reviewed does not justify a broad claim that the market is wide open; it justifies a narrower claim that there is still room for a focused merchant tool if execution is disciplined, scope is tight, and assumptions are tested locally before expansion.
