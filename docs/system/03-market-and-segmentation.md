# 03. Market and Segmentation

## Purpose

Use this file to decide where FastBite can realistically work in the UK and where it should not launch.

## One-Page Summary

- FastBite is most viable in compact UK micro-zones with dense weekday activity, short delivery geometry, and enough merchant concentration to justify a local launch.
- Lunch appears to be the most reliable early demand engine. Breakfast can help in some corridors, but it should not be treated as the default entry wedge across all zones.
- The strongest early zones are likely to be hospital districts, university-adjacent mixed-use zones, strong town or city centres, and selected mixed-use office districts rather than pure office-only areas.
- Merchant pain is clearly operational. Customer priorities are more weakly evidenced but appear to centre on value, reliability, and low-friction ordering. Courier pain is largely about waiting time, route efficiency, and pickup conditions.

## Best Launch Micro-Zones

| Zone Type                           | Why It Works                                                                                                | Main Risk                                                        |
| ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| hospital clusters                   | large year-round staff base, shift patterns, visitor demand, and repeat local ordering behaviour            | site access, parking, and internal navigation friction           |
| university-adjacent mixed-use zones | high mobile adoption, dense repeat demand, and clear lunchtime relevance during term time                   | seasonal demand drops outside term and holidays                  |
| mixed-use central districts         | strong merchant density, short routes, and repeat lunch demand where office attendance has recovered        | Monday and Friday softness, holiday volatility, remote-work drag |
| dense town centres and high streets | broader all-day footfall, merchant concentration, and less dependence on one anchor segment                 | weaker predictability and more variable pickup conditions        |
| transport-adjacent mixed-use nodes  | commuter and passer-by traffic can support breakfast or lunch if surrounded by offices, retail, or campuses | transport hubs alone are too transient and operationally awkward |

## Weak Early Zones

- low-density suburbs
- pure office parks or mono-use business districts with weak Monday and Friday occupancy
- university zones without meaningful year-round non-student demand
- delivery catchments that regularly require long routes and unpredictable pickup times
- areas where dinner is the only strong demand window
- merchant groups without repeat local customers or meaningful marketplace activity already

## Demand Reality

Breakfast alone is unlikely to fund the whole business.

The more realistic pattern is:

- lunch is the main economic anchor in most viable zones
- breakfast is additive in commuter, hospital, and some campus corridors, but is too sensitive to hybrid work, weather, and local routines to anchor the whole thesis
- dinner can be upside where density already exists, but dinner-only zones are a weak starting point for the operating model

Zone demand is also uneven:

- mixed-use office districts are more viable than pure office districts because hybrid work has weakened all-day consistency in many central business areas
- hospital districts are more stable but can be operationally awkward
- university zones can be strong in term time but need explicit seasonality handling
- high streets may be more resilient than expected if footfall and merchant density remain high, but they rely less on breakfast and more on lunch, pickup, and repeat local demand

## Merchant Pain

During peak hours, merchants commonly face:

- multiple active tablets and order sources
- manual re-entry into POS or kitchen flows
- weak control over order pacing
- riders arriving before food is ready
- poor visibility into what is delayed and why
- no clean way to pause, throttle, or mark items unavailable across channels

## Customer Pain

The UK evidence reviewed supports customer concern about value, selection, and reliability more strongly than any one detailed product feature list.

Customers appear to care most about:

- reasonable total cost
- reliable ETA and delivery execution
- simple mobile checkout
- clear status updates when something goes wrong
- usable support when something fails

The recurring frustrations likely include uncertainty, late updates, missed substitutions, and poor exception handling, but these points should be treated as directional unless supported by location-specific customer research or app-review analysis.

## Courier And Pickup Dynamics

The courier-side evidence reviewed is more qualitative than quantitative, but it consistently points to earnings quality and pickup efficiency as the main constraints.

That means:

- slow merchants become unattractive pickup points
- weather, congestion, and parking directly affect dependable supply
- pickup readiness matters as much as rider count

## Competitive Reality

| Platform  | Strength                         | Weakness For Merchants                                   | FastBite Angle                     |
| --------- | -------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Uber Eats | strong demand and brand          | high merchant cost and generic workflow                  | unify orders and grow direct share |
| Deliveroo | strong urban customer experience | expensive for independents and limited operating control | better operational clarity         |
| Just Eat  | broad familiarity                | mixed delivery quality depending on model                | more modern operating layer        |

FastBite should not try to remove all three immediately. It should sit above them first.

## Signals From Public Discussion

Repeated themes from operators and couriers include:

1. effective commission pressure is severe
2. tablet overload is a real operating problem
3. riders avoid merchants with long waits
4. pickup readiness matters as much as dispatch speed
5. merchants want direct ordering only if setup is simple

## Recommended Zone Criteria

Use these as launch filters rather than as claims of market fact. They are evidence-bounded recommendations designed to force discipline.

### Must-Have Conditions

Launch a zone only if all of these are true:

- the zone has a strong weekday daytime anchor, such as a hospital campus, university district, or mixed-use central area with proven office and retail activity
- merchant density is high enough to onboard a tight cluster rather than scattered sites across a city
- typical delivery routes can stay short and operationally predictable
- merchants already feel digital-order fragmentation pain
- local demand is repeatable enough that direct-order migration is plausible over time

### Recommended Measurable Proxies

Use local mapping and fieldwork to score a zone against these proxies:

- anchor population proxy: at least one major daytime anchor with substantial recurring population, such as a major hospital, university cluster, or office-heavy mixed-use district
- footfall proxy: weekday footfall should be materially above the local city average and should not collapse outside Tuesday to Thursday peaks
- merchant density proxy: a pilot zone should contain a tight cluster of suitable QSR, takeaway, cafe, or fast-casual merchants within a walkable operating radius
- marketplace penetration proxy: a meaningful share of target merchants should already be active on at least one major marketplace, because no marketplace activity usually means weaker immediate pain and weaker migration opportunity
- route geometry proxy: most deliveries should be serviceable within a compact radius, with travel times that remain reliable during lunch peaks
- operational access proxy: curbside pickup, rider waiting space, and building access must be workable enough that the zone does not fail operationally even when demand exists

### No-Go Triggers

Do not launch a zone if any of these dominate:

- demand depends mainly on breakfast without a strong lunch base
- the zone is mono-use and visibly weak on Mondays, Fridays, holidays, or term breaks
- merchants are too dispersed to create density benefits
- pickup friction is severe because of parking, campus controls, hospital navigation, or station congestion
- direct delivery requires consistently long routes

## Zone Go / No-Go Checklist

Launch a zone only if most of these are true:

- strong lunch density, with breakfast as upside rather than the main justification
- delivery radius can stay short and operationally stable
- merchants already feel channel fragmentation pain
- repeat demand is local and frequent
- rider pickup conditions are workable

## Open Questions

- Which specific UK micro-zones still combine strong weekday footfall with post-hybrid-work resilience?
- What minimum merchant density and route density make one micro-zone operationally worthwhile?
- Which hospital, university, and mixed-use central districts have the best balance of demand strength and pickup practicality?
- How much Monday, Friday, holiday, and term-break volatility can the business tolerate before a zone becomes unattractive?
